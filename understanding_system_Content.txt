Tropical cyclones are large-scale atmospheric systems that develop over warm ocean waters, characterized by a low-pressure center, strong rotating winds, and organized convective cloud structures. Their formation is influenced by several key environmental conditions, including high sea surface temperatures, high humidity in the lower and mid-troposphere, sufficient Coriolis force, low vertical wind shear, and atmospheric instability. These factors collectively enable the development and intensification of cyclonic circulation, forming well-structured storm systems observable through satellite imagery.

Tropical cyclones can trigger a wide range of hydrometeorological disasters, including floods, landslides, storm surges, extreme winds, and rough seas, which often result in significant economic losses and human casualties. In severe cases, a single cyclone event can impact millions of people by damaging infrastructure, displacing communities, and disrupting livelihoods. For example, events such as Cyclone Senyar in Indonesia demonstrate how tropical cyclones can cause widespread destruction, affecting multiple regions simultaneously and creating long-term socio-economic challenges.

dvorak :
The Dvorak technique is a satellite image–based method widely used to estimate the intensity of tropical cyclones by analyzing cloud patterns such as curved bands, central dense overcast (CDO), and eye structures, which are then converted into intensity values (T-numbers) to represent cyclone strength. This method is extensively adopted because it provides consistent estimations without requiring direct in-situ measurements. For a more comprehensive understanding of its theoretical foundation and development, refer to the official NOAA document: https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/pdf/06velden.pdf

Based on this concept, the pseudomask generator illustrated in the figure adopts the convective structural principles of the Dvorak technique into a computational framework, starting with image preprocessing (cropping), followed by color space transformation from BGR to HSV to separate chromatic and luminance information. Subsequently, structural thresholding is applied using quantitative parameters such as convective core area (A_core), active sector ratio (R_active), band distribution, and symmetry ratio (S_ratio). Based on predefined multiclass thresholds, regions are classified into Red Core (organized cyclone core), Impacted Area (affected regions surrounding the core), Deep Convective Clouds (unstructured convective clouds), and Background. The resulting pseudomask is then utilized as an automatic labeling mechanism to support the training process of a DeepLab-based segmentation model for more accurate detection and structural understanding of tropical cyclones.

deeplab :
DeepLabV3+ is a semantic segmentation model that utilizes atrous convolution and an encoder–decoder structure to capture multi-scale features and refine spatial details. This makes it effective for detecting complex and varied patterns, such as the structural components of tropical cyclones in satellite imagery.

This model is utilized in the cyclone detection system and demonstrates overall solid performance, as reflected in the table above. It performs well in detecting the main cyclone structures, particularly the red core and impacted area, with high detection accuracy and recall under cyclonic conditions. However, performance drops significantly for the DCC class due to its small, scattered, and less structured characteristics, as well as class imbalance. In clear-sky conditions, the model tends to produce false positives on structural classes because of spectral similarities with non-cyclonic convective clouds, resulting in low precision. This indicates that while DeepLabV3+ effectively captures major cyclone structures, challenges remain in detecting small objects, handling imbalanced data, and generalizing to non-cyclonic scenarios.

The multi-class segmentation results are transformed into object-level spatial representations through a structural localization process using bounding boxes. For each class (Red Core, Impacted Area, and DCC), binary masks are generated from the predicted segmentation output, followed by connected component extraction and contour detection to identify distinct regions. These regions are filtered using minimum area thresholds to remove irrelevant small objects. Bounding boxes are then constructed to represent each detected structure, where the Red Core corresponds to the main convective center, the Impacted Area captures the surrounding rainband region, and DCC represents unorganized convective clouds. To better represent irregular shapes, a convex hull is applied to the impacted area, while nested bounding boxes are removed to avoid duplicate detections. This process enables clear and precise identification of the spatial location and extent of cyclone structures directly on the original satellite imagery.

llm :
The LLM interpretation module is designed to generate a concise and structured textual analysis of cyclone detection results based on the spatial outputs from the segmentation model. The system utilizes a large language model accessed via OpenRouter with a free routing mechanism, allowing automatic selection of available models without specifying a fixed architecture.

The input to the LLM is a prompt constructed from the detected bounding boxes, including the number of occurrences and spatial distribution (north, south, east, west of Indonesia) for each class: Red Core, Impacted Area, and Deep Convective Clouds (DCC). The prompt is carefully constrained with explicit rules to ensure that the generated output remains concise, consistent, and aligned with domain knowledge, such as identifying potential tropical cyclone formation only when a Red Core is present.

The LLM then produces a short natural language summary describing the overall weather condition, potential cyclone activity, and associated risks. In this system, the LLM functions purely as a narrative generator, while all analytical decisions are derived from prior rule-based and computer vision processes, ensuring interpretability and consistency of the final output.
